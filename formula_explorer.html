The core problem with the original formula

Dimensional mismatch — big cities always win

recent_count × 1.8 is an absolute count, growing without bound as a market gets larger. The log10(count) × 3.5 density penalty grows logarithmically — far too slowly to offset it. The result: London (800 recent incorporations) gets a recent bonus of 1,440 points, while the density penalty is only 12.9 points. Changing 3.5 to anything short of ~600 makes no meaningful difference.

Fix: use formation rate, not formation count

Replace recent_count × 1.8 with (recent_count / total_count) × 100 — what fraction of the market formed recently. Now a town where 80% of matching companies are new beats one where only 16% are new, regardless of which town is bigger. All four inputs become 0–100 percentages, weights become directly comparable and must sum to 100 to stay interpretable.

How weights should be chosen — three approaches

What the original code did. Weights were chosen so the formula "felt reasonable" on a few mental examples. This produced plausible-looking output but had no guarantee the ranking reflected reality and was dominated by the dimensional problem above.

Changing 3.5 → 6 on the current formula shifts London's score from 1,457 to 1,450. It's noise. The bug is structural, not a constant.

What this feature uses now. Define 4–6 archetypes whose correct ranking you'd agree on, then set weights until the formula produces that order. No labeled data needed.

  1. Write down the archetypes (e.g. "TinyBoom should beat London even though London is larger")
  2. Use the sliders below to tune until the ranking matches your judgment
  3. Record the weights and document why the ordering is correct
  4. Re-run whenever market context changes

Limitation: the "correct" ordering is still a judgment call. It won't generalize to archetypes you haven't considered.

The principled long-run goal. Collect 50–100 labeled examples — areas you assessed as high/low opportunity and later verified. Fit weights by regression.

  1. Use the dashboard over 6–12 months, flagging areas with a 0–10 opportunity rating
  2. Follow up: did those areas show formation growth / competitor entries / customer traction?
  3. Feed the outcome labels + the five normalized features into a Ridge regression (regularized to avoid overfitting with a small sample)
  4. The fitted coefficients become your weights — code: sklearn.linear_model.Ridge(alpha=1.0).fit(X_normalized, y_labels)
  5. Re-fit every 12–18 months or when market context shifts

Limitation: requires real labeled outcomes, which take time to collect. "Opportunity" also depends on your time horizon.
